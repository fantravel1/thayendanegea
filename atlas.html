The Interactive Atlas

Places That Built the Story

An interactive map of the Mohawk Valley, battle sites, diplomatic journeys, settlement regions, and memorial locations — from 18th-century Haudenosaunee homeland to the Grand River and across the Atlantic.

Key Places

Canajoharie

c. 1743 — Birthplace Region

Upper Mohawk Castle — the community where Thayendanegea spent his early years.

Fort Johnson / Johnson Hall

1740s–1770s

Sir William Johnson's estate. Gateway to colonial diplomacy.

Fort Stanwix

1768 Treaty / 1777 Siege

Site of the pivotal 1768 Treaty and later Revolutionary War siege.

Six Nations of the Grand River

1784 — Haldimand Tract

The land grant that became the largest First Nations reserve in Canada.

Burlington Bay

1800–1807

Thayendanegea's final residence where he spent his last years.

London, England

1775–1776 & 1786

Two diplomatic missions — met the King, sat for the Romney portrait.

Brantford, Ontario

Present Day

Named in his honor. Home to the Chapel of the Mohawks.

Fort Niagara

1759–1780s

British post and refugee center during the Revolutionary War.

Newtown (Elmira)

August 1779

Battle site during the Sullivan-Clinton Campaign.

Cherry Valley

November 1778

A controversial engagement of the frontier war.

Woodland Cultural Centre

Present Day

Preserves and promotes First Nations culture and heritage.

Historical Reference

The Territory, c. 1720

Before European encroachment accelerated, the Haudenosaunee Confederacy controlled a vast territory. This map shows the approximate territories of the Six Nations.

The Eastern Door

The Mohawk — Thayendanegea's nation — held the eastern door of the Longhouse, serving as first contact with European newcomers.

The Central Fire

The Onondaga kept the central fire of the Confederacy. Their territory was the seat of the Grand Council.

The Western Door

The Seneca guarded the western door. Their vast territory stretched toward the Great Lakes.
